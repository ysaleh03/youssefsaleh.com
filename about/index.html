university

I graduated with a BSc. Honours in Computer Science from the University of British Columbia in 2026. My honours thesis was titled Optimized Verification Condition Generation for Rust Verification and supervised by Professor Alexander J. Summers.

Some of my favourite courses were CPSC 311, CPSC 312, CPSC 320, CPSC 411, CPSC 420 and CPSC 421, as well as CPSC 539-S and CPSC 509.

research

In 2026, I am working with Professor Alexander J. Summers at UBC's Software Practices Lab on developing a new prototype implementation of Mendel, a technique for verifying clients of interior-mutable Rust modules that uses implicit capabilities to describe the permissions granted by the design of module APIs.

In 2025, I worked with Professor Alex Summers on an optimized encoder for Prusti, a program verifier for Rust based on the Viper verification infrastructure. This project introduced a novel Purified encoding that translates Rust programs into Viper using only pure, snapshot-based representations. This project later became my honours thesis.

In 2024, I did a directed study with Professor Mark Greenstreet on formal verification. My work focused on proving the equivalence of tail-recursive and non-tail-recursive depth-first search on Huffman trees using ACL2. You can read the report here.

During my undergrad, I worked as an Undergraduate Teaching Assistant for CPSC 320 (Intermediate Algorithm Design and Analysis), CPSC 311 (Definition of Programming Languages), CPSC 312 (Functional and Logic Programming) and CPSC 210 (Software Construction).

interests

My academic interests lie broadly in theoretical computer science, with a particular focus on programming languages, formal verification and theory of computation.

Over the past two years, I've become familiar with some proof assistants like ACL2, Lean and Viper. I am intrigued by F*, Agda and Rocq, and want to gain experience with using them as well.

When it comes to programming paradigms, I vastly prefer functional. Most of my (functional) experience is with Haskell, Racket and Erlang, though a few months with Common Lisp have convinced me I'd enjoy most lisps. I also greatly enjoy Rust — multiparadigm as it is.

Beyond that, I enjoy going down Wikipedia rabbit holes as much as the next person, especially when they're about historical linguistics.
